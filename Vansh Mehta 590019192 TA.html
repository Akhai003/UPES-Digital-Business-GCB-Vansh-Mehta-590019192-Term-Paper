01 Introduction

The global trading system is currently undergoing its most significant stress test since the founding of the General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade (GATT) in 1947. We have transitioned from an era of "Hyper-Globalization" to an era of "Geoeconomic Fragmentation."

The World Trade Organization (WTO), established on January 1, 1995, represents the culmination of efforts to create a binding, rules-based international order. However, the paralysis of the WTO Appellate Body has led to a regression from a rules-based system to a power-based system. This paper analyzes this shift through the lens of three core agreements: GATT (Goods), GATS (Services), and TRIPS (Intellectual Property).

02 Background of Agreement

Unlike the provisional GATT 1947, the WTO is a permanent institution with legal personality. Its founding principle is the "Single Undertaking" — nothing is agreed until everything is agreed.

"The Single Undertaking"

A country cannot sign up for GATT (to export shirts) without also signing up for TRIPS (protecting patents) and GATS (opening banking sectors).

03 Trade Patterns Before & After

Post-1995, global trade volume exploded due to the predictability offered by the WTO. However, post-2015, we are witnessing a "Stagnation of Trade Liberalization." Tariffs are no longer falling; they are rising under the guise of National Security and Environmental Protection (e.g., Carbon Taxes).

04 Winners and Losers

The Winners

  • Developed Nations (EU/US): Through the Agreement on Agriculture (AoA), they successfully protected their farmers using the "Green Box" subsidies while restricting developing nations.
  • Big Tech: The GATS moratorium on electronic transmissions has allowed tech giants to export software and digital services duty-free globally.

The Losers

  • Developing Nations (India): The "Amber Box" restrictions in AoA limit India's ability to offer Minimum Support Price (MSP) for food security.
  • Generic Manufacturers: TRIPS product patent rules delayed the entry of affordable generic medicines, impacting public health in the Global South.

05 Implications for Businesses

Supply Chain Resilience

The WTO's MFN protection is eroding. Managers must shift from "Just-in-Time" efficiency to "Just-in-Case" resilience. The China + 1 strategy is now a risk management necessity.

Green Compliance

The EU's Carbon Border Tax will tax Indian Steel & Aluminum. Exporters must implement carbon accounting immediately or face a 25% penalty.

Data Localization

With Digital Sovereignty rising, data flows are restricted. Firms must use local cloud zones (AWS Mumbai) to comply with India's DPDP Act.

06 Case Studies of Companies

ICT Sector Impact

Case Study: Mobile Manufacturing (DS582)

"How Apple, Samsung, and Xiaomi are navigating India's Import Tariffs"

The Policy Shift

To support the "Make in India" (PLI) scheme, India imposed 20% customs duties on imported smartphones. The EU sued India at the WTO (DS582), arguing this violated the ITA-1 agreement.

Impact on Firms

The tariffs forced global giants like Apple and Samsung to shift assembly to India (e.g., Foxconn plants in Tamil Nadu) to avoid the 20% duty. This validates the "Tariff-Jumping FDI" theory.

The Outcome

Although the WTO ruled against India in 2023, India appealed to a non-functional Appellate Body. This strategic maneuver allows the tariffs to remain, protecting the domestic manufacturing ecosystem of companies like Dixon Technologies.

07 Policy Challenges & Criticisms

1. The TRIPS Inequity

The most significant criticism of the WTO is its handling of the Covid-19 pandemic. India & South Africa proposed a waiver of IP rights to produce vaccines.

The Failure:

The EU/UK blocked the waiver to protect pharma profits. A watered-down deal was reached in 2022, widely criticized as "too little, too late."

2. The Digital Fiscal Cliff

Since 1998, the WTO has banned tariffs on digital transmissions. This moratorium expires in 2026.

The Challenge:

Developing nations lose billions in revenue as trade shifts from physical goods (taxable) to digital downloads (tax-free). India is leading the block to end this moratorium.

08 Conclusion & Recommendations

"The World Trade Organization is not dead, but it is dormant. We have transitioned from an era of Global Integration to an era of Geopolitical Fragmentation. The strategy for the next decade is not free trade, but managed trade."

Managerial Rec 1

Leverage bilateral FTAs (like India-UAE CEPA) for market access rather than relying on WTO MFN rates.

Managerial Rec 2

Diversify sourcing immediately. The era of single-source efficiency (China) is over; risk mitigation is key.

Managerial Rec 3

Prepare for Non-Tariff Barriers. Compliance with ESG and Carbon norms will be the new "Visa" for exports.
